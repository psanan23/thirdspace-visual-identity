Open decision · palette re-direction

Four palettes, all with confidence.

The v2 palette read too neutral, too melancholy. Pulled from your moodboard: Goop's warmth, Vogue's editorial cover, the Loewe Foundation's gallery cobalt. Each direction keeps cream as the base and B&W for interview portraits — the colour does the lifting on covers, pull quotes, and atmospheric posts. Fonts (Fraunces + Karla) and wordmark (Fraunces stacked) carry across all four.

Where it wins: the cobalt-terracotta complementary pair is one of the most editorial colour combinations in modern publishing — La DoubleJ, vintage Vogue covers, the late-2010s wave of Brooklyn editorial brands all use it. Tobacco keeps the system from looking like a colour-block experiment. Mustard, used once every nine posts, gives the feed a third moment that isn't blue or orange.

How a 9-post feed reads: 3 cream-background covers · 1 cobalt pull-quote · 1 terracotta pull-quote · 2 B&W portraits (inside Story Sets) · 1 atmospheric · 1 mustard featured moment. The eye moves across warm → cool → warm without anything fighting.

Risk: this is the richest direction; it needs the most editorial discipline. One feature colour per Story Set, picked at planning. Never two cobalts side by side. Never terracotta + mustard on the same post.

Pillar guardrail: ✓ Balanced. Cobalt → ambition/work register. Terracotta → domestic/unsaid register. Mustard → identity/becoming register. Tobacco neutralises across all four. No single subject's Story Set gets coded to one pillar — the colour rotates with the editorial calendar, not with the theme.

How to pick

Reply with a letter (A–D), or "A but with B's cobalt added" style if you want to mix. Whatever lands, I'll update the kit's CSS variables once — every page reflects the new palette.

Pillar-guardrail flag (per CLAUDE.md): A leans warm/domestic. B leans cool/ambitious. C leans gallery/identity. D is the most pillar-balanced. If we want pillar testing to stay broad in the first 12 weeks, D — or A with cobalt added from B — keeps the platform from coding to one woman.

06 — Photography

iPhone-led, never iPhone-looking.

Every portrait is shot on iPhone Pro Max. That's a constraint, not an excuse. This page tells you how to make it work.

Placeholder · v2 first pass. The full shooting and editing guide (subject framing, light, white balance, presets) is the largest open piece in the kit. This page captures the principles that are already locked. The next iteration of this kit fills in the practical "how to shoot" details.
Principle 01

No front-facing faces. Unless she said yes.

Default to behind, side, hands, the room she's in. Front-facing is the exception, requested in writing, never assumed. This protects the woman and creates a distinctive visual signature for the feed.

Principle 02

The image should not be the best photo of her.

Every featured woman picks three to five images that represent this season of her life. Not headshots. Not "best of." What she values, what she's becoming, the texture of her actual days. These become the basis for her interview, her visual direction, and her Story Set.

The brief to her: "Send me three to five images from your camera roll — not necessarily of yourself — that feel like this season of your life. The window you look out of. What's on your desk. The book you keep returning to. Something you almost deleted but didn't."
Principle 03 · locked v2.1

Colour goes on type. B&W goes on imagery.

This is the operating rule for everything photographic. Every image — Portraits AND Atmospheric — is black and white. Colour only appears on typographic posts (Cover, Pull quote, Editor's note). The feed reads as colour blocks alternating with B&W imagery. No colour grading on photos. No matching atmospheric photos to the palette. Sustainable from day one.

Why this rule wins on consistency: the hardest open question in the kit was "how do we colour-grade atmospheric photos consistently on iPhone?" Answer: we don't. We make them all B&W. The colour palette lives where it's controllable — Canva templates, typography blocks — and the imagery stays unified by treatment, not by hue. Same logic Goop uses on their highlight covers: format consistency, not colour-matching.
Composition rule

One subject. Lots of negative space.

Since colour can't unify the imagery (everything is B&W), composition must. Every photographic post — portrait or atmospheric — follows the same compositional rule: one clean subject, breathing space around it, no busy backgrounds.

Composition · yes

  • A hand holding a coffee cup, two-thirds of the frame empty.
  • A figure walking away, framed by a doorway.
  • An open notebook on a marble counter, single shaft of light.
  • A vase on a sideboard, nothing else visible.

Composition · no

  • Three objects on a desk competing for attention.
  • Wide street shot with multiple people.
  • Centered subject with everything in equal focus.
  • Anything that looks like a "lifestyle stock photo."
Photography rules — locked

What's already decided.

  1. Every photographic post is B&W. Portrait and Atmospheric.
  2. Colour only appears on typographic posts (Cover, Pull quote, Editor's note).
  3. No front-facing faces without explicit consent.
  4. The image is not "the best picture of her." It's a frame she chose.
  5. One clean subject. Lots of negative space. Never busy backgrounds.
  6. No white studio backdrops. Ever.
  7. Stock and AI imagery is allowed only on Atmospheric posts (and still B&W).
  8. No heavy edits — no skin smoothing, no portrait mode, no obvious presets. If you can tell which iPhone preset was used, the edit is wrong.

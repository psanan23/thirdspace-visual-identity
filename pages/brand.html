Manifesto — opening prose

There is a woman looking for a version of herself in the media she consumes.

She has ambition. Perhaps a career she still cares about. A point of view. A sense of taste built over years. And now, a life that includes children.

She is not just trying to "balance" motherhood with everything else, because she knows "having it all" is an illusion. She is trying to understand who she is becoming now that motherhood has shifted the shape of her life.

Third Space was built for her.

Audience — consumer-facing

The approved paragraph.

Use this verbatim on the website, Instagram, or any public-facing surface. It's already been pressure-tested.

You didn't stop having a career the moment you had children. You're building something, raising someone, and still figuring out what you want from the next decade. There's pressure to make it look like you've worked it out. You haven't, not all of it. Third Space is for the women living that — and for the women willing to say so out loud, so others know they're not the only one.

What every post must do

The three jobs.

Every post does at least one. Ideally two. If a post doesn't do any of these, it's not Third Space.

Job 1

Normalise the mess.

"I am not the only one whose life no longer fits a neat script."

Job 2

Expand possibility.

"There are many legitimate ways women are choosing to do this."

Job 3

Satisfy curiosity.

"How does she actually make that work day to day?"

Without Job 3, it becomes moodboard feminism. Without Job 1, it becomes aspirational pressure. Without Job 2, it becomes a mirror with no windows.

Tone guardrail

What we're not.

This is not a platform for women who have figured it out. The brand should never read as "the performance of having arrived." The audience is mid-journey. Content that implies she should have it together by now is off-brand.

She is looking for

  • Sharper language for things she already feels.
  • Practical inspiration that sparks her to decide differently.
  • Women who look, talk, and think like someone she'd want as a friend.
  • Real trade-offs, named honestly.

She is not looking for

  • Motivational quotes.
  • Mum tips, parenting hacks, playdate content.
  • Girlboss content. Funny mum memes.
  • Inspiration that turns into pressure to do even more.
Reference brands

What we pull from, and what we don't.

Stated explicitly so AI tools and collaborators know the reference frame.

Luminary Mothers — PRIMARY

Closest global equivalent. Editorial Q&A platform for ambitious mothers. We pull: editorial register, woman-led portraiture, content as recognition. We don't pull: their roster (different geography).

Aesop — AESTHETIC

Pull: warm muted palette, type-led restraint, empty space as a design element. Don't pull: high-production studio photography we can't replicate on iPhone.

Goop — EDITORIAL CUE

Pull: clean editorial type, the way interviews stand out from the feed (their B&W treatment is a precedent for our portrait approach). Don't pull: their wellness vocabulary, their colour overload.

Spread the Jelly — COMMUNITY CUE

Pull: people-led discovery, faces as the entry point to community. Don't pull: the cluttered, multi-format grid. We optimise for legibility.

What we're not pulling from Spotify: motion-heavy formats, the logo bug, music licensing, the Gen-Z entertainment register. What we are: the framework structure (mandatory vs flexible elements), the do/don't pattern, the tone-of-voice approach, the "template library" concept.
